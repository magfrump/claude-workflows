## Gotchas

- **The "three-line header" phrase appears in eight places** [observed by grep]: step 2 header preamble (line 64), step 2 prose paragraph after the bullets (line 70 — "three named anchors"), step 2 Done-when (line 116), step 3 introduction (line 125), step 3 header preamble (line 127), step 3 Done-when (line 266), step 5 prose (line 302), step 5 Done-when (line 305), and step 6 freshness check (line 318). - **"Considered and discarded" specifically, not just "alternative"**: the word "discarded" matters because it forces a verdict — the alternative isn't just listed, it's marked as *rejected*. A weaker phrasing like "also considered" would let the author dodge the move that creates the framing accountability. [inferred — informed by how "considered and rejected" lands in design docs vs. how "also considered" tends to read as decoration]
- **No plausible alternative is the diagnostic**: if the researcher can't name one alternative framing, the framing hasn't actually been examined. The spec should call this out explicitly — a placeholder like "Considered and discarded: none" is a signal to do the framing work, not a permit to skip it.
- **Framing vs. goal collision risk**: authors may write "users can't export reports" as both the Goal and the Problem framing's problem clause. That's fine — Goal is the *outcome the loop pursues* ("add CSV export"), Problem framing names the *problem the loop is responding to* ("users can't export reports"). They're related but distinct framings of the same situation. Worth one example in the spec.
- **Body sections already exist for framing-related content** — What exists / Invariants / Prior art / Gotchas can absorb deeper framing exploration. The header line is just the *visible* commitment; it doesn't replace body-level framing work, it pulls the headline of that work onto the front page where re-readers can see it.

## Why this matters (problem statement)

A stale problem framing is the most expensive failure mode in a research-driven workflow. Everything downstream — the goal, the plan, the tests, the implementation — is shaped by the framing. If the framing is wrong, every artifact built on it is wrong, and the cost of discovering this late scales with how much of the downstream chain has been completed. The current header surfaces Goal (the *outcome* of framing) but not the framing itself, so a misframed loop can pass every Self-check (step 5) and Done-when criterion without the framing ever being re-examined.

The cheapest move that catches misframing is to make the framing visible on the header where it's re-read on every glance — and to require naming *one* discarded alternative, because the discarded alternative is the diagnostic that the framing was actually examined rather than defaulted to. The cost is one line per research doc; the benefit is making framing drift externally visible the same way the cite-line made project-state drift externally visible.
